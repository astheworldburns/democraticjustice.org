# Democratic Justice Copy Style Guide

Updated: March 25, 2026

## Purpose

Democratic Justice should sound severe, calm, exact, and useful.

The site should not ask for trust through tone. It should earn trust through structure, sourcing, and plain language.

## Core Rule

Write so the reader can answer three questions quickly:

1. What is this?
2. What can I do here?
3. Why should I trust it?

## Voice

### Target voice

- severe
- calm
- exact
- disciplined
- unseduced by its own rhetoric

### Avoid

- cleverness for its own sake
- breathless framing
- over-curated seriousness
- academic throat-clearing
- machine-polished reassurance
- reaction-shaped positioning

## House Law

### Punctuation

- No em dashes in evergreen brand copy.
- Prefer period, colon, comma, or parentheses.

### Framing

- No “not this, but that.”
- No house style built on opposition.
- Use contrast sparingly and only when strategically necessary.

### Sentence rules

- One idea per sentence.
- One claim per line when possible.
- No sentence over 25 words unless legal or factual precision requires it.
- No paragraph over 3 sentences on evergreen pages.

### Vocabulary

Prefer:

- read
- check
- search
- cite
- follow
- open
- compare
- name
- publish
- document
- verify

Use abstract nouns carefully. If a sentence contains one abstraction, the rest of the sentence should be concrete.

### Trust

- Show proof through structure, not hype.
- Do not explain seriousness. Practice it.
- Cut any sentence that sounds like it wants applause for being responsible.

## Patterns To Cut

### Ban or review aggressively

- durable
- accountability
- institutional
- framework
- structure
- structured
- authority
- verification
- consistency
- mechanics
- architecture
- ecosystem
- transparency
- grounded
- rooted
- publication-wide
- inferential
- foundational
- axiomatic

These words are not forbidden forever. They are high-risk. Use them only when they name a real process or record.

### Ban these patterns

- “not this, but that”
- “rather than”
- “instead of” when used as brand posture
- “no X, no Y, no Z” as default rhetoric
- abstract noun stacks
- adverb-heavy pressure
- manifesto language in utility pages
- technical stack talk in primary brand copy

## Preferred Constructions

### Use declarative sentences

Bad:

> Stories are written for clarity rather than the churn and posture of social feeds.

Good:

> Stories are written for clarity and rereading.

### Use concrete reader value

Bad:

> Full-text search runs entirely on the static site, so readers can move through the archive without relying on an external search service or a database-backed app.

Good:

> Full-text search is built into the site so readers can move through the archive directly.

### Use verbs over institutional abstraction

Bad:

> The authority of the work comes from verification, clarity, and consistency rather than personality alone.

Good:

> The work should stand on sourcing and editing, not personality.

### Use plain proof language

Bad:

> every foundational fact and every inferential step

Good:

> the key facts and the reasoning

## Brand Vocabulary To Keep

These ideas are central and should remain visible in plain language:

- proof
- records
- source documents
- named responsibility
- archives
- search
- West Virginia
- accountability reporting
- permanence
- citation value

## Evergreen Page Guidance

### Homepage

- Lead with the story.
- Explain the mission in one short paragraph.
- Keep standards language operational, not theoretical.

### About

- State what the publication covers.
- State how readers can use the work.
- State why the publication is built this way.

### Archive / Search / RSS

- Explain the function first.
- Keep utility pages free of manifesto tone.
- Do not talk about the stack unless the reader benefit is immediate.

### Footer

- Keep it short.
- Say what the publication publishes.
- Offer contact and navigation.

## Pass/Fail Checklist

Before shipping evergreen copy, check:

- Does this sentence use a verb where a noun stack used to sit?
- Does this page tell the reader what they can do here?
- Does any line sound impressed with its own seriousness?
- Is the trust claim visible in the structure, not just in the wording?
- Can any sentence lose 5 words without losing meaning?

If the answer is yes, rewrite.
